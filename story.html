Why we built Awe Well

Awe has always been part of my life. I wanted the people I love to have more of it.

I'm Sloane. Awe has always been a part of my life, for whatever reason it stuck, though never as much of it as I would like.

What I started noticing was that the people I love were rarely tapping into it at all. Not because anything was wrong with them. Their lives were full, and busy, and the wonder had quietly been squeezed out to the edges. I knew what it had done for me, so I went looking for ways to help them find more of it.

The more I read, the bigger the thing got. Awe was not a nice extra. It turned out to be a key: to better mental wellbeing, to handling stress, to the part of you that keeps perspective when life is pressing in. That is the point where helping a few people I love turned into building this.

Awe Well is that work, made shareable. A way for you to rekindle your own relationship with awe.

It started as a document, not an app: twenty pages arguing that most of us are walking past the best part of the day. That document became the spec. Not a feature list, a set of beliefs. That everyday awe beats the rare and scenic kind. That noticing is a skill you can get back. And that almost everything built in this category asks too much of people who are already tired.

What we refused to build

Most of the early decisions were subtractions. Every one of them costs us something a growth team would want, and every one of them is the reason the app is worth keeping on your phone.

  • No streaks. A streak turns a good day into an obligation and a missed day into a failure. Guilt is the opposite of wonder, and it is the single most common reason people quietly delete apps like this one.
  • No scores, no levels, no progress bar. There is nothing here to get good at. You noticed something today or you did not, and tomorrow arrives either way.
  • No feed, no friends, no sharing by default. The moment noticing becomes performance, it stops working. What you write is for you.
  • Nothing that fakes it. The recordings are real: a humpback from NOAA, lightning at Jupiter from NASA, dawn over Yellowstone from the National Park Service. No stock ambience, no generated nature sounds.

The day it has to work for

The longest section of that original document was not about wonder. It was about the days you cannot feel it.

Grief, burnout, the flat grey stretch after something hard. An app that only works when you are already open to being moved is an app that abandons people exactly when they need it. Being told to find the beauty in your day, on that day, is worse than being told nothing.

So there is a quiet path. On any day, one line under the invitation offers something gentler instead: put both feet flat on the floor and feel it hold you. It has been holding you all day without being asked. Those prompts never leave your phone, because that state is nobody's business but yours.

A practice that only works on good days is not a practice. It is a decoration.

Small promises, kept

Every invitation in the library was written and read aloud before it shipped, because a prompt that sounds like a form letter breaks the spell.

Your reflections are never used to train a model. Speak instead of type and your voice becomes text on your phone, where the recording is never kept. The app will not send you outside when the weather is dangerous, and it checks the forecast before it does, because an invitation you should not take is worse than none.

What comes next

A voice. Not narration over a screen, but the practice out loud: invitations read to you on a walk, longer guided moments, and eventually a conversation, where you say what you noticed and get asked one more question, then another, until you find why it landed.

The document said that noticing is only half of it. The other half is making sense of what you noticed, and that has always been easier out loud than on a page.
